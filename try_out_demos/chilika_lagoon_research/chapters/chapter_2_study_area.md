# Chapter 2: Study Area

## 2.1 Overview of Chilika Lagoon

Chilika Lagoon, located on the eastern coast of India, is the largest coastal lagoon in the country and a wetland of international importance recognized under the Ramsar Convention. This brackish water lagoon spans approximately 1,165 square kilometers, delineated by its dynamic interface with the Bay of Bengal and a network of estuarine channels that facilitate complex hydrological exchanges. Chilika plays a crucial role in sustaining regional biodiversity, supporting diverse habitats including mangroves, mudflats, and seagrass beds that provide vital ecosystem services (Research Data).

## 2.2 Physical Geography and Hydrology

The lagoon's geomorphology is characterized by a sandbar system that separates it from the sea, with multiple tidal inlets regulating saline water inflow. Its waters undergo seasonal fluctuations influenced by monsoonal rainfall, freshwater influx from rivers such as the Daya and Bhargavi, and tidal dynamics from the Bay of Bengal. This results in spatial and temporal heterogeneity in salinity gradients and sediment distribution, shaping the lagoon's ecological zoning. The hydrological regime supports a mosaic of aquatic habitats, facilitating the survival of various aquatic and avian species (Research Data).

## 2.3 Ecological Significance

Chilika Lagoon serves as a critical habitat for numerous flora and fauna, including migratory waterfowl, endangered species, and commercially significant fishery resources. Its mangrove ecosystems act as natural buffers against coastal erosion while mediating nutrient cycling and carbon sequestration. The lagoon's rich biodiversity underpins local livelihoods centered on fishing and aquaculture, making it an ecological and socio-economic keystone in the region (Research Data).

## 2.4 Environmental Challenges

Despite its ecological importance, Chilika Lagoon faces considerable environmental pressures. Anthropogenic influences such as encroachment, pollution from agricultural runoff, industrial effluents, and sedimentation have impaired water quality and habitat integrity. Episodes of eutrophication and algal blooms, driven by nutrient loading, threaten aquatic life and disrupt trophic interactions. Additionally, alterations in hydrological connectivity due to infrastructural developments have impacted salinity regimes and sediment transport, exacerbating ecological degradation (Research Data).

## 2.5 Seasonal and Temporal Variability

The lagoon's water quality exhibits marked seasonal variability tied to monsoon cycles influencing nutrient inputs, turbidity, and chlorophyll-a concentrations. Satellite-based monitoring from 2019 to 2024 reveals fluctuations in turbidity and chlorophyll levels corresponding to changes in freshwater discharge and sediment dynamics. Such multi-temporal analyses are imperative for understanding long-term trends and facilitating adaptive management of the lagoon's ecosystems (Research Data).

## 2.6 Relevance to Water Quality Monitoring Studies

The complex interplays of physical geography, ecological richness, and environmental stressors make Chilika Lagoon an ideal natural laboratory for advancing water quality monitoring methodologies. Innovations integrating remote sensing and machine learning have been motivated by the need for scalable, cost-effective approaches to capture this spatiotemporal complexity. The lagoon's diverse habitats and anthropogenic challenges provide critical testbeds for validating spectral indices and predictive models of water quality parameters, supporting sustainable lagoon management (Research Data).
